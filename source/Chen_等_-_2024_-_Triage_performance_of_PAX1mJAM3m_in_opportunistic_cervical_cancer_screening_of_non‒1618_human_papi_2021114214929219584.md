# Abstract

Objectives In this study, we aimed to validate the performance of the PAX1 and JAM3 methylation (PAX1m/JAM3m) test as a triage tool for detecting cervical intraepithelial neoplasia grade 3 or worse $( \mathsf { C } | \mathsf { N } 3 + )$ in non-16/18 high-risk human papillomavirus-positive patients (non-16/18 hrHPV +). 

Methods The triage performance of liquid-based cytology (LBC) and the PAX1m/JAM3m test for detecting CIN3 + were compared. 

Results In total, 1851 participants had cervical histological outcomes and were included in the analysis. The sensitivity/specifcity of the LBC test results with atypical squamous cells of undetermined signifcance or worse $( \mathsf { L B C } \geq \mathsf { A S C U S } )$ and the PAX1m/JAM3m test were $9 0 . 1 \% / 2 6 . 7 \%$ and $8 4 . 8 \% / 8 8 . 5 \% ,$ respectively. $P A X 1 ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ had the highest diagnostic AUC (0.866, $9 5 \%$ confdence interval (CI) 0.837–0.896) in the whole cohort. All cancers $( \mathsf { n } = 2 0 )$ were detected by $P A X 1 ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ . Compared with LBC ≥ ASCUS, PAX1m/JAM3m(+) reduced the number of patients who needed referral for colposcopy by $5 7 . 2 1 \%$ $7 4 . 6 6 \%$ vs. $1 7 . 4 5 \%$ ). The odds ratios for detecting $C | N 3 + \mathrm { b y }$ $\begin{array} { r } { \mathsf { L B C } \geq \mathsf { A S C U S } } \end{array}$ and $P A X 1 ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ were 3.3 $9 5 \%$ CI 2.0–5.9) and 42.6 (27.1–69.6), respectively $( \mathsf { p } < 0 . 0 0 1$ ). The combination of LBC ≥ ASCUS or $P A X 1 ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ slightly increased the diagnostic sensitivity $( 9 8 . 0 \% )$ $9 5 \%$ CI: 95.8–100%) and referral rate $( 7 7 . 0 9 \% )$ ) but reduced the diagnostic specifcity $( 2 4 . 8 \% , 2 2 . 7 - 2 6 . 8 \% )$ $2 4 . 8 \% ,$ . 

Conclusions In non-16/18 hrHPV(+) women, PAX1m/JAM3m was superior to cytology for detecting $\mathsf { C N 3 + }$ . Compared with LBC ≥ ASCUS, PAX1m/JAM $3 ^ { m } ( + )$ reduced the number of signifcant referrals to colposcopy without compromising diagnostic sensitivity.

# Introduction

Cervical cancer (CC) is a major public health issue afecting women globally and ranks fourth among cancers causing fatalities in females [1]. With advancements in vaccination, screening, and treatment [2], the risk of cervical cancer has been signifcantly reduced in recent years by early detection and treatment of precancerous cervical lesions [3, 4]. Additionally, cervical cancer screening and the triage of individuals with abnormal screening results are the most important aspects in the secondary prevention of cervical cancer [5, 6]. 

Current HPV screening strategies are limited by the overdetection of clinically irrelevant, transient HPV infections, which can result in unnecessary follow-up visits, colposcopy overreferral, and overtreatment [7]. Te World Health Organization (WHO) recommends initial screening with HPV DNA testing in combination with specifc triage methods and regular screening every 5 to 10  years [8, 9]. Te lower specifcity of HPV testing is currently countered by refex triage testing with (repeat) cytology, especially in non-16/18 hrHPVpositive patients [7, 10]. Currently, the guidelines of the American Society for Colposcopy and Cervical Pathology (ASCCP) and the Chinese Society for Colposcopy and Cervical Pathology (CSCCP) directly refer to HPV16/18- positive women for colposcopy [11]. For non-16/18-type HPV infections, the guidelines recommend incorporating results from liquid-based cytology (LBC) testing for further consideration. Colposcopy and biopsy are recommended for LBC-positive (atypical squamous cells of undetermined signifcance (ASCUS) or worse $( \geq \mathrm { A S C U S } )$ individuals, whereas follow-up is advised for LBC-negative patients [12]. However, LBC results are highly susceptible to the infuence of subjective factors, signifcant variability between diferent pathologists and laboratories, and stringent training requirements [13, 14]. It is also afected by sampling, preparation, patient age, and the lesion itself [15]. Consequently, cervical cancer caused by non-16/18 hrHPV infections is the focus of future screening and treatment eforts. 

Many studies have provided increasing evidence that DNA methylation testing can be a promising cervical screening method because of its good performance, reproducibility, and ability to triage hrHPV-positive $\mathrm { ( h r H P V + ) }$ women [16–23]. Te PAX1 gene, which contains a paired box domain and a paired-type homeodomain, is a member of the paired box (PAX) family. Te PAX1 gene plays a crucial role in spinal cord development and embryogenesis [24]. A higher PAX1 methylation level promotes the deterioration of lesions and induces tumorigenesis, such as in cervical, oral, and esophageal cancer [25, 26]. JAM family members belong to the immunoglobulin superfamily and play important 

roles in maintaining tight junction integrity, regulating cell migration, and determining cellular polarity [27, 28]. Te methylation status of the host genes PAX1 and JAM3 has great potential for the triage of hrHPVinfected individuals in cervical cancer screening [16, 22, 23, 29, 30]. Previous studies have shown that, as a secondary triage modality for $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ women, the sensitivity and specifcity of PAX1 methylation status alone or PAX1 and JAM3 in combination with detection of other genes for cervical intraepithelial neoplasia grade 3 or worse $\left( \mathrm { C I N } 3 + \right)$ can reach more than $8 0 \%$ [31, 32]. In addition, the triage efectiveness of DNA methylation testing is comparable to that of cytology, and even methylation triage can reduce the number of referred patients [10, 16]. For $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ triage, the PAX1/JAM3 methylation $( P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } )$ ) detection kit (CISCER®) has been approved by the China National Medical Products Administration. However, its non-$1 6 / 1 8 ~ \mathrm { h r H P V ( + ) }$ triage efectiveness has not been adequately explored. 

To validate the triage efectiveness of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ in non-16/18 hrHPV(+), we enrolled participants from two prospective cohorts who received liquid-based cytology (LBC) testing, hrHPV testing, and $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ testing for opportunistic cervical cancer screening. Non-16/18 $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ participants were selected, and the triage efcacy of LBC with $\geq$ atypical squamous cells of undetermined signifcance $( \mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S } )$ and $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ for detecting CIN3 + was assessed. 

# Materials and methods

# Participants and study design

Te participants were from two multicenter studies conducted from November 2020 to December 2021 (METHY3) and from May 2022 to October 2022 (METHY4). Eligible women underwent opportunistic cervical cancer screening consisting of hrHPV genotyping, LBC testing, and $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ testing. Te triage performance for detecting $\mathrm { C I N } 3 + \mathrm { i n }$ non-16/18 $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ women was compared according to the cervical histology results obtained via colposcopy or other surgical procedures. 

Te inclusion criteria for the two cohorts were the same and consisted of the following: 1) aged 18 years or older; 2) intact cervix; and 3) no serious immunodefciency conditions, such as HIV infection, history of organ transplantation, or treatment with immunosuppressive drugs. Participants were excluded if they had known malignant tumors of the female genital tract or active malignant tumors of other sites still under treatment. All study subjects volunteered to participate in this study and signed an informed consent form. 

# Sample collection, cytologic pathology assay, and hrHPV genotyping

All eligible participants underwent gynecological examinations and collection of cervical cytology data. Te cytology samples were subjected to hrHPV and cytological pathology assays. Te residual samples were sent for $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ testing. 

All the samples were subjected to high-performance HPV testing via Cobas® 4800 assays (Roche Diagnostic Products, Shanghai, China). Te Cobas HPV test was carried out according to the manufacturer’s instructions [33]. Te Cobas HPV test is an in vitro polymerase chain reaction-based method for quantitative detection of high-risk HPV DNA and can detect HPV16, HPV18, and non-16/18 hrHPV (31, 33, 35, 39, 45, 51, 52, 56, 58, 59, 66, and 68) of high-risk HPV types. 

Te LBC testing was used for cytology tests (Hologic, Inc., MA, USA). Cytologists used the 2014 Bethesda system [34] to classify LBC results. LBC diagnoses were classifed as no intraepithelial lesions or malignancy (NILM), atypical squamous cells of undetermined signifcance (ASCUS); low-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (LSIL); atypical squamous cells, cannot exclude high-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (ASC-H); high-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (HSIL) and squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) in this study. 

# DNA methylation assays

Methylation detection was performed in a certifed DNA laboratory, and the operators and staf members were blinded to the clinical information, LBC testing, HPV genotyping, and cervical histopathology results of the patients. Genomic DNA (gDNA) was extracted from the exfoliated cervical sample with the JH-DNA Isolation and Purifcation Kit (OriginPoly Bio-Tec Co., Ltd., Beijing, China) per the manufacturer’s instructions. Te DNA concentration was quantifed with a NanoDrop 2000c spectrophotometer (Termo Fisher Scientifc, DE, USA). Briefy, $2 0 0 { - } 1 0 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n g }$ of gDNA was subjected to bisulfte conversion by means of JH-DNA Methylation-Lightning MagPrep (OriginPoly Bio-Tec Co., Ltd., Beijing, China). Te levels of $P A X I ^ { m }$ and $J A M 3 ^ { m }$ were subsequently determined with the CISCER® DNA Methylation Detection Kit for Cervical Cancer (real-time PCR) with glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) as the internal control (OriginPoly Bio-Tec Co., Ltd., Beijing, China) by means of the ABI 7500 real-time PCR system platform (Life Technology, Foster City, CA, USA) per the manufacturer’s instructions. Te hypermethylation level of the PAX1 gene and JAM3 gene was determined by the diference between the two Ct values (ΔCt $P A X I = \mathrm { C t }$ PAX1-Ct GAPDH and ΔCt $J A M 3 = \mathrm { C t }$ JAM3-Ct 

GAPDH). According to the manufacturer’s instructions, a positive CISCER $( P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } )$ test result was defned as a ΔCt PAX1 ≤ 6.6 or $\Delta \mathrm { C t } J A M 3 \leq 1 0 . 0$ . 

# Histology evaluation

Cervical histology samples were collected by colposcopy performed according to current guidelines [35, 36] or by other surgical procedures, such as conization, the loop electrosurgical excision procedure (LEEP), or hysterectomy for cervical lesion indications. Te results of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ were not considered indications for colposcopy referrals or cervical biopsy in this study. 

# Statistical analysis

SPSS 26.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA) and R (version 4.1.2, Vienna, Austria) were used for all the statistical analyses. Te $\mathtt { X 2 }$ test was used for comparisons between groups. A normality test was used to determine whether the variance of the population was equal to that of the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test. Nonnormally distributed data are expressed as M (Q1, Q3), and comparisons between two groups were performed with the nonparametric Mann‒Whitney U test. Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves were used to evaluate the AUCs of diferent detection methods between ${ < } \mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ patients and $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ patients. Te sensitivity, specifcity, positive predictive value (PPV), and negative predictive value (NPV) for detecting $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ lesions were calculated with $9 5 \%$ confdence intervals (CIs). Te sensitivity and specifcity for detecting $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ lesions between diferent triage methods were compared by means of using McNemar’s test. All tests were two-sided, and P values < 0.05 with $9 5 \%$ CIs indicated statistically signifcant diferences.

# Performance of $\mathsf { L B C } \geq \mathsf { A S C U S }$ and PAX1m/JAM3m in detecting $\mathsf { C l N 3 + }$

Table 2 shows the diagnostic accuracy, sensitivity, specifcity, and colposcopy referral percentages of various triage methods and their combinations across the whole cohort. Compared with that of $P A X I ^ { m } ( + )$ or $\mathrm { J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + ) }$ alone, the detection efciency of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ in combination for CIN3 + was improved in women who were not $1 6 / 1 8 \mathrm { \ h r H P V }$ positive.

Among all screening strategies, $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ triage resulted in the lowest colposcopy referral percentage $( 1 7 . 4 5 \% )$ , which was signifcantly lower than the colposcopy referral percentage of LBC ≥ ASCUS $( 7 4 . 6 6 \% )$ . 

To investigate whether the combination of the two methods improves the detection of $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ , we combined triage of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ with $\mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S } .$ , which led to an increased sensitivity of $9 8 . 0 \%$ $9 5 \%$ CI: $9 5 . 8 \mathrm { - } 1 0 0 \% )$ , but the specifcity decreased to $2 4 . 8 \%$ $9 5 \%$ CI: $2 2 . 7 \mathrm { - } 2 6 . 8 \%$ ), and the referral percentage increased to $7 7 . 0 9 \%$ .

# Discussion

In this study, we investigated the clinical utility of the $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ assay as a triage test for non-16/18 $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ patients in routine cervical screening. Te ΔCt values of PAX1 and JAM3 methylation decreased as histopathology severity increased. Furthermore, in this study, the triage capabilities of various detection methods for cervical cancer screening in non- $1 6 / 1 8 \mathrm { h r H P V ( + ) }$ patients were comprehensively evaluated. Tese results indicate that the use of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ from cervical epithelial cells is highly accurate in detecting $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ lesions with a sensitivity that is similar to that of LBC tests but with signifcantly higher specifcity, thus greatly reducing colposcopy referrals. 

Te prevalence of high-risk HPV types varies geographically. In parts of Asia, HPV31/33/52/58 are reported as frequently as HPV16 or HPV18 in precancerous lesions [37]. According to a study in southern Shanghai, China, in terms of the total proportion of hrHPV infections, there were more non-16/18 hrHPV infections $( 6 8 . 2 2 \% )$ than HPV16/18 hrHPV infections. Non-16/18 hrHPV infection accounts for $5 0 . 8 4 \%$ of patients with HSILs or cervical cancer (HSILs +) [38]. On the basis of the results of this study from screening hospital-based gynecology clinics, we found that the prevalence of non-16/18 infections was $1 5 . 7 \%$ (4735/30084) of the total screened population. Because non-16/18 hrHPV types are associated with a higher prevalence of infection, the triage of non-16/18-positive women may be more efective in identifying high-risk patients. Moreover, as non-$1 6 / 1 8 \ \mathrm { h r H P V }$ infections account for a higher proportion of patients with HSILs or cervical cancer, there is a greater need for an efective management strategy that identifes and triages high-risk women so that preventive measures can be developed and implemented to reduce the incidence of these types of HPV-associated cervical cancers. Cytology, an option for the triage of $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ women, not only results in an increased burden of care due to increased colposcopy referrals but also may result in unnecessary overdiagnosis [11, 12]. In this study, cytology demonstrated low specifcity, potentially infuenced by the designated positive threshold. In this study, we aimed to evaluate various detection methods for identifying $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ patients. Te elevated positivity rates of CIN1 and CIN2 by cytology are shown in Table 1. Tis contributes to the reduced specifcity of cytology for detecting $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ . Tese fndings suggest that cytology may not be an efective triage tool and that it is limited in accurately identifying high-risk individuals among non-16/18 HPV-positive women. Another limitation of cytological 

analysis in the triage of non- $1 6 / 1 8 ~ \mathrm { h r H P V ( + ) }$ patients is that despite the $9 0 . 1 \%$ sensitivity of LBC for the detection of $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ , there were still four cases of cervical cancer that were not detected by cytology. Tese patients were asked to undergo colposcopy for further evaluation because of abnormal vaginal bleeding, suggesting that in some cases, liquid-based cytology may miss some cases. In contrast, methylation testing was capable of identifying all the cervical cancer samples. 

In this study, the diagnostic accuracy of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ was similar to that of previously reported methylation tests [39]. Previous studies on the application of methylation for triage in $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ patients have shown that methylation has a diagnostic ability equal to or even superior to that of cytological pathology. As reported, the $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ sensitivity of FAM19A4/miR124-2 methylation analysis was similar to that of cytology $( 7 1 . 3 \%$ vs. $7 6 . 0 \%$ ), with a lower specifcity ( $7 8 . 3 \%$ vs. $8 7 . 0 \%$ ) [40]. E6/ E7 detection combined with PAX1 methylation is superior to HPV testing for cervical cancer screening, and the AUC of PAX1 (0.919) is signifcantly higher than those of HPV (0.541) and E6/E7 detection (0.607) $( \mathbf { p } < 0 . 0 0 0 1 )$ [41]. CADM1/MAL methylation in combination with cytology triage of $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ patients was better than cytology alone, and combined triage revealed a sensitivity of $8 6 . 8 \%$ vs. $6 5 . 8 \%$ for $\mathrm { C I N } 3 ( + )$ and a corresponding specifcity of $6 4 . 8 \%$ versus $7 8 . 6 \%$ [42]. To investigate whether combining LBC and $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ testing improves the efcacy of $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ detection. We performed a comparison and found that the combination of the two methods did not enhance the detection efcacy for $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ . In other words, the triage of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ alone was not only superior to cytology testing alone but also preferable to a combined program of both methylation and cytology $( P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ vs. $\mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S } ( + )$ vs. $\mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S }$ and/or $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ , AUC: 0.866 vs. 0.584 vs. 0.836/0.614). Te combined $\mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S }$ and $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ triage revealed a $\mathrm { C I N } 3 ( + )$ sensitivity of $9 8 . 0 \%$ , which was greater than the $9 0 . 1 \%$ for $\mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S }$ and the $8 4 . 8 \%$ for $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ . Recent studies have shown that the DNA methylation levels of host cell genes and viral genes increase with the severity of CIN and are very high in cervical cancer patients [43–46]. Tese fndings suggest that methylation testing may be more efective in diagnosing more severe cervical lesions. Tis suggests that methylation testing could be more efective in diagnosing more severe cervical lesions, whereas cytology might fail to be as useful. Tis fnding also suggests that morphological reliability is still not a substitute for the accuracy of molecular markers and that diferences in methylation levels between cytologic types do not account for diferences in histopathology. 

As a triage method for non-16/18-positive women, the application prospects of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ are promising. Compared with $\mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S }$ in this study, $P A X I ^ { m } /$ $J A M 3 ^ { m }$ was more efective in reducing colposcopy referrals $( 1 7 . 4 5 \%$ vs. $7 4 . 6 6 \%$ ). Tese fndings are similar to the results of a previous study of methylation for hrHPV triage [32], where both $P A X I ^ { m }$ and $Z N F S 8 2 ^ { m }$ reduced colposcopy referrals when $\mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S }$ were compared $3 0 . 2 \%$ , $2 6 . 9 \%$ vs. $5 0 . 7 \%$ , respectively). Our study revealed a signifcant odds ratio for ≥ CIN $^ { 3 + }$ for LBC≥ASCUS and $P A X I ^ { \mathrm { m } } / J A M 3 ^ { \mathrm { m } }$ . Tese results suggest that among non-16/18 $\mathrm { \ h r H P V ( + ) }$ women, $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m } ( + )$ patients are more likely to need direct colposcopy than follow-up. 

Tis study also has certain limitations. First, the sample sizes were uneven across centers. Second, evaluations and considerations of the timing and extent of high-risk human papillomavirus (hrHPV) infection were lacking in our study. No patients were followed up, and the risk of short- or long-term progression could not be distinguished between patients with $\mathrm { L B C } \geq \mathrm { A S C U S }$ and patients with $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ . Future prospective studies with large sample sizes in diferent regions and adequate follow-up data are needed to confrm our fndings. 

# Conclusion

In non-16/18 hrHPV(+) women, $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ was superior to cytology for detecting $\mathrm { C I N } 3 +$ with favorable diagnostic sensitivity and specifcity and signifcantly reduced referrals to colposcopy. Te combination of $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ and cytology was not more advantageous than $P A X I ^ { m } / J A M 3 ^ { m }$ alone for triage.
